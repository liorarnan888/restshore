# Voice, Tone, and SEO Guidelines

Research date: 2026-03-19

## Voice principles

RestShore should sound:
- warm
- competent
- specific
- calm
- encouraging
- never patronizing
- never shaming
- never inflated

The voice should feel like a trusted sleep coach who knows CBT-I well enough to be exact, but speaks like a human.

## Language rules

### Use these terms
- CBT-I
- sleep efficiency
- wake anchor
- sleep window
- stimulus control
- wind-down
- sleep diary
- sleep coach
- sleep log
- clinician summary
- protected sleep window

### Prefer these plain-language translations
- "sleep restriction" -> "sleep efficiency training" or "protected sleep window"
- "stimulus control" -> "use the bed for sleep, not wakeful activities"
- "cognitive restructuring" -> "reframe sleep thoughts" or "offload the worry"
- "counter-arousal" -> "downshift"

### Avoid these
- generic "sleep hygiene" as the whole solution
- "miracle"
- "fix your sleep in one night"
- "you failed"
- "the user" in user-facing copy
- language that sounds clinical but not useful

## Messaging rules

- Explain why before asking for effort.
- Show the plan as a system, not a list of random tips.
- Make the user feel like the calendar is personalized for them.
- Keep the tone consistent across intake, report, emails, and future pages.
- When possible, connect a recommendation to the user's own answer pattern.

## Evidence and claim policy

Safe claims:
- CBT-I is the gold standard / strongly recommended behavioral treatment for insomnia when sourced from authoritative guidance.
- CBT-I is evidence-based and multi-component.
- A diary helps tailor the plan.

Claims to avoid or soften:
- guaranteed outcomes
- universal results
- overprecise statistics without context
- treating personalization as if it is individualized medicine

## Content architecture recommendations

### Homepage
- explain what CBT-I is
- show the six-week arc
- demonstrate personalization
- show trust and clinician friendliness

### Report page
- summarize the problem in plain English
- show the plan
- explain the logic
- show how to use the calendar

### Doctor summary
- use concise clinical language
- include dates, patterns, adherence, and red flags
- avoid marketing phrasing

### Check-ins
- write like a guide, not a survey
- keep the questions short
- make the answer choices intuitive

## Copy examples

Good:
- "Your sleep window is the treatment. The calendar helps you follow it."
- "One rough night does not reset the plan."
- "We only change the future when the pattern repeats."

Bad:
- "We will fix your sleep fast."
- "Answer a few questions about last night" on a page that already says check-in.
- "The user should do X"
